On a standard device like the Adafruit Metro ESP32-S3, the Wi-Fi radio is a single piece of hardware. It cannot be in two "states" at the exact same time.

Why they conflict
To understand the limitation, think of the radio like a walkie-talkie:

Station Mode (STA): The ESP32 is "talking" to your router. It stays on your home's specific Wi-Fi channel (e.g., Channel 6) and constantly exchanges "handshakes" to stay connected to Home Assistant.  

Monitor Mode: The ESP32 becomes a "passive listener." To find your Pixel 6 Pro, it must "hop" through every channel (1 through 11 or 13) to catch the phone's probe requests.

The moment the ESP32 hops away from your home's Wi-Fi channel to scan others, it loses its connection to your router. Home Assistant will immediately report the device as "Offline."

The 3 Ways to Solve This
If you want to use an ESP32 as a sniffer without losing your Home Assistant connection, you have three professional-grade workarounds.

1. The "Dual-Radio" Setup (Recommended)
This is the most stable method. You use two cheap ESP32s instead of one expensive one.

ESP-A (The Sniffer): Runs in Monitor Mode. It "sniffs" the air for your Pixel's MAC address. It is not connected to Wi-Fi.

ESP-B (The Bridge): Connected to your home Wi-Fi and Home Assistant.

How they talk: Connect the TX/RX pins of ESP-A to ESP-B. When ESP-A "sees" your phone, it sends a simple serial message to ESP-B, which then tells Home Assistant.

2. The Ethernet Workaround
If you use an ESP32 board with a built-in Ethernet port (like the WT32-ETH01), you can connect the board to your network via a cable.

Data: Sent over Ethernet to Home Assistant.

Radio: The Wi-Fi radio is now 100% free to stay in Monitor Mode indefinitely without ever needing to connect to an Access Point.

3. The "Serial-to-Pi" Method
Since you mentioned having a Raspberry Pi 5, you can plug your ESP32 into the Pi via USB.

The ESP32: Stays in Monitor Mode and "sniffs" the air.

The Connection: It sends the data over the USB cable (Serial) to a small script running on your Pi.

The Update: The Pi then sends that data to Home Assistant (either via MQTT or the HA API).
